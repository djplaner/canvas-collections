5254LAW Course Outline and Syllabus

Welcome to Administrative Law

Administrative Law is the body of law regulating the ways in which government operates. It is about the rules and limits that apply to not only the operations of the Crown, Cabinets, Ministers, government departments, and municipal corporations but also the various administrative tribunals and agencies deployed by governments for the carrying out of governmental functions of all kinds. It is concerned with the procedures by which all these various instruments of government operate, the substantive scope of their mandates, and the remedial structures that exist to ensure that decision-makers of various kinds act in accordance with the rule of law.

Throughout this course you will be encouraged to reflect upon the divide between public law and private law and, in particular, the circumstances under which governmental authorities of various kinds or in various capacities are subject not to the special regime of Administrative Law principles and remedies but to the private law rules of contract, tort, restitution and the like.

There is some small overlap between this subject and Constitutional Law. However, Administrative Law is not about the policing of the divide between federal and provincial jurisdiction enshrined in the Constitution Act,1867 and the other statutes that constitute the Canadian constitution. Nor does it concern itself in detail with the constitutional incidents of the Crown, Parliament and the various legislative assemblies, or the executive branch.

The primary questions considered in this course are:

  1. The circumstances under which governmental decision-makers are subject to an obligation of procedural fairness to those affected by their decisions, and, where applicable, the content of that obligation.
  2. The extent to which the substantive decisions of administrative decision-makers are subject to merits scrutiny by the courts on the basis of concepts such as error of law, error of fact, and abuse of discretion, and if so, what is the standard of review that reviewing courts bring to bear.
  3. The remedial framework within which the superior courts, both federally and provincially, exercise their review powers.

Module 2

Procedural Fairness: Right to an Impartial Decision Maker and Right to be Heard

In this week, we will build on our introduction to procedural fairness, which is one of the two organising concepts around which our course is structured (the other being substantive review). Procedural fairness is entirely focussed on the way in which the decision is made, not the result obtained. When looking at issues of procedural fairness, the question is not whether the result was "fair" but rather whether the processes provided an individual with a fair hearing we will take a further look at procedural fairness including both the right to be heard and the right to be heard by an impartial decision maker.

This will involve further examination of some of the issues covered in the workshop that arise in the Baker framework, including delving deeper into the issues affecting threshold, the Baker 5 factors, and case law explaining the various aspects of the level of procedural content.

  • Topic 1 – Procedural Fairness and the Right to an Impartial Decision Maker (Bias, Institutional Independence, Institutional Decision Making, Guidelines, and Rule Making)
  • Topic 2 – Procedural Fairness and the Right to be Heard (sources, threshold, and general content)

Module 4

Introduction to Substantive Review - From Dunsmuir to Vavilov

This week marks a shift to the second part of our course, which will focus on the issue of substantive review. We are leaving procedural fairness behind and embarking into an exciting area of law that is in a state of evolution. Late in 2019. the Supreme Court of Canada set out a new framework for how courts are to review the substantive decisions of administrative decision makers. To begin, we will explore the law as it existed prior to 2019. This will help us to understand what the Supreme Court of Canada was attempting to address when it created the framework in Vavilov. The Supreme Court was very responsive to criticism in this case, and undertook to provide clarity and address the concerns of jurists and academics alike when it passed the new framework.

Importantly for our purposes though, the decision was not a revolution. The Vavilov framework is an evolution in the law on substantive review and it is therefore important to understand the case law that preceded the decision in that case.

Moreover, under the previous Dunsmuir framework and under the current Vavilov framework, the foundational principles and goals are the same. The ideas from the first part of our course — the rule of law, the separation of powers, the courts ability to review executive action, as well as efficiency and access to justice within the administrative law realm — continue to be instructive as we turn to substantive review.

  • Topic 1 — The road to Vavilov: Dunsmuir and its progeny
  • Topic 2 — The Vavilov framework: Understanding the current approach to substantive review

Module 5

Legislative Standards and Review of Charter Decisions

During this week, we will look at two discrete topics. The first ties off our discussion from the previous week looking at standards of review. In addition to the common law framework set out in Vavilov, there are also legislative standards that exist. As not much time has passed since the Supreme Court's decision in Vavilov, legislative standards reflect the previous framework (some even pre-date Dunsmuir). However, given the hierarchy, legislative standards continue to displace common law frameworks and this remains an important part of determining the standard of judicial review.

We will then turn to the thorny issue of administrative decisions and the Charter. The Supreme Court of Canada has held that administrative decision makers can make determinations of Charter issues. However, given they often lack legal training and are not experts on constitutional interpretation, how these decisions should be reviewed by courts has been a difficult issue for the courts to work out. Several recent high profile cases have also made this a fascinating area of law to explore.

  • Topic 1 — Substantive review and legislative standards
  • Topic 2 — Judicial review of Charter determinations
