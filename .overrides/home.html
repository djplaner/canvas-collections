Improve your Canvas site's design

Canvas Modules are the Canvas LMS' main tool for organising learning activities. Canvas Collections introduces new functionality to address long known limitations with Canvas Modules.

Collections Canvas modules limitation Solution
collections Challenges structuring content (2015) due to the linear design (2016) of modules. Organise modules into different collections of modules that suit your design intent. Users can navigate between different collections, focusing only on the current collection.
Representations The modules user interface "is a design nightmare, presenting information as one big list/outline". More visual interfaces like tiles or cards (2022) have been requested and extensions developed (2017). Having more control over the visual representation requested to visualise priorities (2020), improve visual cues, and enable the modules page to act as a course home page (2016). For each collection, choose from a number of different representations. Or, develop a new one specifically for your purpose.
Objects There is no ability to provide connective test (2022) or other module specific meta-data to help scaffold student engagement with modules. Transform vanilla modules into course specific objects by associating additional meta-data with each individual module. Meta-data that can modify the visual appearance of the modules page through the chosen representation.
  • one long overwhelming list;
  • cannot embed contextual information; and,
  • lack visual appeal.

Canvas Collections helps address these limitations.

Compare and contrast

Canvas Modules   Collections   Representations   Objects   Claytons   Simple to configure

A course with 11 modules. A long scroll. A need to remember where things are. Little visual distinction beyond use of emojis in module names. Little design or contextual information visible.

11 modules now grouped into three, design specific collections. Navigate between collections.

A long requested ability from the Canvas Community to group modules based on design intent.

Rather than a long scroll of neat textual boxes, represent a group of modules using one of a growing set of representations. A representation you can change at any time simply by selecting another option.

With little ability to associate design/context specific information with a module all a student sees is yet another vanilla Canvas module. Nothing to easily distinguish between a lecture, an assessment, a resource, or some other domain specific resource.

Collections allows you to associate additional design specific information. That information is used to change what the students see. Making it easier for them to recognise the purpose.

Even if you can't get your institution to install Collections, you can still use Collections to address the above problems.

"Claytons" Collections helps you follow populate Canvas community advice to create create an inviting course home page. A Canvas page your students can use without Collections.

Improve your students' learning experience and outcomes

Findability improves self-efficacy & motivation

Findability has been found to be the only and most significant predictor of student motivation and self-efficacy (Simunich et al, 2015; Crews et al, 2017).

Simunich, B., Robins, D. B., & Kelly, V. (2015). The Impact of Findability on Student Motivation, Self-Efficacy, and Perceptions of Online Course Quality. American Journal of Distance Education, 29(3), 174–185. https://doi.org/10.1080/08923647.2015.1058604

Aesthetics & usability improve outcomes & motivation

Heidig et al (2015) found that perceived aesthetics and usability positively affected learner emotional states. Contributing to small improvements in learning outcomes and larger impacts in intrinsic motivation.

Heidig, S., Müller, J., & Reichelt, M. (2015). Emotional design in multimedia learning: Differentiation on relevant design features and their effects on emotions and learning. Computers in Human Behavior, 44, 81–95. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2014.11.009

Course organisation improves retention

Mulijana & Luo's (2019) systematic literature review found that course organisation improves student satisfaction and motivation, and promotes cognitive presence.

Muljana, P., & Luo, T. (2019). Factors Contributing to Student Retention in Online Learning and Recommended Strategies for Improvement: A Systematic Literature Review. Journal of Information Technology Education: Research, 18. https://doi.org/10.28945/4182

Flexibility necessary for scale

Achieving scale in education requires designs that can flexibly adapt to effective use in a wide variety of contexts across a spectrum of learners and teachers.

Clarke, J., & Dede, C. (2009). Design for Scalability: A Case Study of the River City Curriculum. Journal of Science Education and Technology, 18(4), 353–365. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10956-009-9156-4
